## 🖥️ Human Computer Interaction · SECV2113

Human Computer Interaction fundamentally changed the question I ask when building something. Before this course, the question was "does it work?" Afterwards, it became "can a real person use this without frustration?" The course covered usability principles, user research methods, prototyping, and heuristic evaluation.

Conducting usability evaluations showed me how consistently even well-intentioned designers overestimate what users already know. The gap between how a developer experiences their own system and how a first-time user experiences it is almost always larger than expected. I left with a user-centred mindset that I now bring to every interface I design — and to the way I structure data outputs and dashboards so that the people relying on them can actually act on what they see.

Deepening my practical skills with tools like Figma and conducting real user testing sessions remains on my development list, as lived experience with users is something no textbook can fully replicate.

## 📊 System Analysis and Design · WBL · SECP2613

This course taught me that the work before the code is often more important than the code itself. Requirements gathering, feasibility analysis, use case modelling, and system design documentation formed the backbone of a process that I had previously skipped entirely by jumping straight into implementation.

The Work-Based Learning component made the lesson real in a way lectures alone cannot. Working through actual organisational system requirements showed me how iterative and negotiation-heavy the analysis phase truly is. Stakeholders rarely arrive with clear requirements — they arrive with problems, and it is the analyst's job to translate those problems into specifications that developers can act on. That skill, I now understand, is one of the rarest and most valuable in any technical team.

## 🕌 Appreciation of Ethics and Civilizations · ULRS1182

This course traced the moral frameworks of major world civilisations — Islamic, Eastern, and Western — and explored how they converge on shared values of justice, honesty, and responsibility despite vastly different philosophical origins.

What resonated most was the idea that no technology is value-neutral. Every system reflects choices made by its designers, and those choices carry moral weight. This course gave me a broader vocabulary for thinking about those choices — one that goes beyond legal compliance into genuine ethical reasoning. As a data engineering professional, I carry that vocabulary into decisions about data collection, privacy, and the downstream uses of the systems I build.

## 💻 Programming Technique II · SECJ1023

The step from Programming I to Programming II was steeper than I anticipated. This course moved into territory that required a fundamentally different relationship with the machine — pointers, dynamic memory allocation, recursion, and file handling demanded that I think carefully about what is actually happening in memory, not just what appears in the output.

Recursion in particular required me to trust the logic rather than trace every step manually — a mental shift that was uncomfortable at first and transformative once it settled. The struggles in this course made me a more precise and humble programmer. I developed a clearer sense of where my understanding was solid versus where I had been approximating, and I came out of it with a more honest relationship with the code I write.

## ⚙️ Computer Organization and Architecture · SECR1033

This was the most challenging course of my first year, and I say that with full honesty. Assembly language, CPU architecture, memory hierarchies, and pipelining operate at a level of abstraction far removed from high-level code — every step is explicit, every register matters, and there is nowhere to hide imprecision.

The difficulty itself was instructive. Tracing instructions through a CPU made me genuinely appreciate what compilers, operating systems, and runtime environments do on a programmer's behalf — work so automatic it is easy to take for granted. I came away with a deeper respect for performance-conscious programming and a much clearer mental model of the relationship between software and hardware. That foundation has continued to shape how I think about resource efficiency in data pipelines and distributed systems.

I plan to revisit assembly language and CPU pipelining deliberately — not because my career requires it daily, but because the depth of understanding it produces is valuable in ways that surface unexpectedly.

## 📈 Probability and Statistical Data Analysis · SECI1143

Statistics initially felt like a detour from the core computing curriculum. By the end of the semester, I understood it was actually central to everything I would do as a data engineer. Probability theory, distributions, hypothesis testing, and regression are not auxiliary tools — they are the formal language for reasoning about data quality, uncertainty, and the validity of analytical outputs.

The course challenged me mathematically, but it also reframed what "understanding data" actually means. A pipeline that produces results no one can statistically validate is not a complete pipeline. I now think of statistical literacy as a professional standard, not an optional add-on, and I want to keep building it through practical application in Python and R rather than limiting it to textbook exercises.
